The tale of sikki stems can be traced back from the early days of the Vedic era in India when it was practiced in the middle of the nomadic existence. The indigenous people of the area startedexperimenting with the available natural resources. Subsequently, Mithila women begancreating sikki grass utensils to store grains and other items. Earlier, proficiency in Sikki craft was also a mark of a female qualification.

Uniqueness

A craft dominated by women, sikki is distinguished by its long grass and brilliant golden hue. This craft not only
provides income and employment to the women but also empowers them. Every item can be made in dozens of
shapes and can be dyed in a variety of colors. The grass is weaved together, constructing a sculptural form, all of it
using just one tool with a 6-inch long needle known as "Takua". Moreover, about ten types of weaving techniques are
used in Sikki craft and the coiling technique, which is the oldest, and is also commonly used. Like madhubani, sikki is
also folk in nature, and was prompted by a prevalent Mithila social custom.

Traditional Motifs

Motifs and patterns used in products are majorly influenced by religion, customs, nature and beliefs of the region where the artisan comes from. They give the product a new life and an individualistic identity and are also enjoyed as aesthetically appealing artworks. Most popular motifs in sikki products are flowers, leaves, peacocks, stars, sun and swastik.
